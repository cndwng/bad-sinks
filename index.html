Part II:
Everyday Sinks

The Anatomy of a Sink

Every sink can be broken into five components worth evaluating: Faucets, Water, Basins, The Surroundings, and The Innards (I'm not a plumber, I don't care).

Basins

Design Factors

  • Shape
  • Depth
  • Slope
  • Drainage
  • Cleanability
  • Placement: inset or above the counter

Basins: Bad Shapes

Bad basin design is everywhere. Hard angles are hard to clean. Too-small basins are useless. Gross stuff collects in the bottom when there's no slope toward the drain.

Examples of bad basin shapes

Basins: Troughs

Mixed feelings. The slope for drainage is great. But how do you clean this thing?

The trough sink: a study in conflicting affordances

Faucets

Handle Design Factors

  • Type of handle: automatic sensor, push down, dual handles, single handle
  • Ease of temperature control
  • Can it be turned off without hands?

Push Down Faucets

I feel like I mostly see these in rest stops and schools.

Pros: You don't have to turn it off. Mechanical trigger (not reliant on a sensor). Wastes less water.

Cons: They are literally never set up correctly. They are on for about 4.5 seconds — just long enough for you to soap up and get the whole thing soapy and wet when you push it again.

Automatic Sensors

Pros: Wastes less water. Feel high tech. Don't have to touch a dirty handle.

Cons: Also often not set up correctly. Some of them are impossible to trigger. Length of time is sometimes too short. No temp control.

Faucets: Spouts

  • Water flow / shape (e.g. shower, jet stream)
  • Distance from basin
  • Angle of water

These all affect whether you end up with a splash disaster or a pleasant hand-washing experience.

The Surroundings

Design Factors

Really you just need space to actually put things.

Consider the context:

  • Home bathroom sinks need enough space for toothbrushes, soap, etc.
  • Kitchen sinks need space nearby for dishes, etc.

This is a bar bathroom sink (the pointy one from earlier). It literally wasn't designed to have space for your hand to get soap.

The most offensive sink design error:

When your hand is too close to the back of the sink and you can't actually open your hands to scrub — and then your fist bumps the back of the dirty sink.

Faucet Too High

If the faucet is too high up, the water will splash onto the countertop on the back of the sink. The whole counter will always be wet.

Appendix A: Hand Dryers

Design factors: Height, Depth (distance from wall), Trigger, Length of time.

Bad Design™:

  • Too tall
  • Too close to wall
  • Automatic sensors that are impossible to trigger
  • Default lengths of time where your hands are still soaking wet and you have to wave your hands like an idiot until it turns off
