Part I:
Design

I’m a product designer. I spend most of my working hours thinking about how digital interfaces communicate with people — what a button should look like, whether a flow makes sense, how to make software feel obvious to use.

The physical world is a whole different story. Nobody redesigns the faucet every sprint. Bad physical design just sits there, permanently, being bad, forever.

Sinks in particular have been a low-key obsession of mine for years. After reading The Design of Everyday Things (more on that in a second), I started photographing bad sinks everywhere I went. Bathroom at a bar? Photographed. Museum restroom? Photographed. My optometrist’s office? You already know.

This is what I found.

with all of that out of the way, let’s get started.

design ≠ art

The Design of Everyday Things

For those who don’t know: The Design of Everyday Things by Don Norman was originally published in 1988 under the more accurate title The Psychology of Everyday Things. Norman — a cognitive scientist who’s had stints at Apple and UC San Diego — uses cognitive psychology to explain why some things are intuitive and others are inexplicably infuriating.

Most importantly, it popularized user-centered design: the principle that if you can’t figure out how to use something, it’s not your fault. The design failed you.

It’s been a standard design textbook for decades. I read it for two different college courses. Don Norman is basically Design Daddy.

The book introduced (or at least popularized) the concept of affordances — the idea that objects communicate how they want to be used. A push plate says push. A door handle says pull. When the signal and the correct action don’t match, you get bad design.

Norman door
noun  /ˈnɔːr·mən dɔːr/

A door that gives the wrong signal about how to interact with it — most commonly, a door that should be pushed but has a pull handle, or vice versa. The visual cue (the handle) contradicts the correct action (push), so users repeatedly choose wrong. Then feel embarrassed about it. Even though it is not their fault.

Named after Don Norman, who used poorly designed doors as a central example in The Design of Everyday Things. If a door needs a sign that says “PUSH,” it is a Norman door.

More Examples

Norman doors are just the most famous example. This problem is everywhere.

Stove knobs: Most stoves arrange four burners in a 2×2 grid, but the knobs sit in a row with no spatial relationship to what they control. You need that little printed diagram just to boil water. Any design that requires its own decoder key is a design that has already failed.

Light switch panels: A row of identical switches, no labeling, no indication of which controls which fixture. You flip them in sequence until something happens, then forget which was which by the next day. The switches share no visual relationship with the lights they control — a problem of mapping, or rather, the total absence of it.

TL;DR — good design does its job and is easy to use.

Functional first, beautiful second.

Part II:
Everyday Sinks

The Anatomy of a Sink

Every sink can be broken into five components worth evaluating: Faucets, Water, Basins, The Surroundings, and The Innards (I’m not a plumber, I don’t care).

Basins

Design Factors

  • Shape
  • Depth
  • Slope
  • Drainage
  • Cleanability
  • Placement: inset or above the counter

Basins: Bad Shapes

Bad basin design is everywhere. Hard angles are hard to clean. Too-small basins are useless. Gross stuff collects in the bottom when there’s no slope toward the drain.

Basins: Troughs

Mixed feelings. The slope for drainage is great. But how do you clean this thing?

Faucets

Handle Design Factors

  • Type of handle: automatic sensor, push down, dual handles, single handle
  • Ease of temperature control
  • Can it be turned off without hands?

Push Down Faucets

I feel like I mostly see these in rest stops and schools.

Pros: You don’t have to turn it off. Mechanical trigger (not reliant on a sensor). Wastes less water.

Cons: They are literally never set up correctly. They are on for about 0.3 seconds — just long enough for you to soap up and get the whole thing soapy and wet when you push it again.

Automatic Sensors

Pros: Wastes less water. Feel high tech. Don’t have to touch a dirty handle.

Cons: Also often not set up correctly. Some of them are impossible to trigger. The on-time is sometimes too short. No temp control.

Spouts

  • Water flow / shape (e.g. shower, jet stream)
  • Distance from basin
  • Angle of water

These all affect whether you end up with a splash disaster or a pleasant hand-washing experience.

The Surroundings

Design Factors

Really you just need space to actually put things.

  • Home bathroom sinks need enough space for toothbrushes, soap, etc.
  • Kitchen sinks need space nearby for dishes, etc.

Bad Design™:

  • Too close to wall
  • Automatic sensors that are impossible to trigger
  • Default run times where your hands are still soaking wet and you have to keep waving like an idiot until it turns off
